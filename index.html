Why does pricing matter?

The profit formula
Profit = Sales Price × Units sold − Variable Cost × Units sold − Fixed Cost
Profit = SP × X − VC × X − FC
SP = Sales Price — what you charge per unit X = Units sold — how many you sell VC = Variable Cost — cost per unit (materials, delivery…) FC = Fixed Cost — costs that don’t change (rent, salaries…)

Step 2 — the real world: all three levers at once
Apple: the master class in combined pricing power
In reality — and outside our mathematical simulation designed to isolate the impact of each lever — companies can pull all three levers simultaneously. No brand has demonstrated this more deliberately than Apple.

As a price leader, Apple has consistently raised prices across product categories. The introduction of premium tiers (iPhone Pro Max) raised average selling prices while the retention of lower-priced models (standard iPhone, iPhone SE, iPad Air) made entry-level options feel comparatively affordable, boarding new consumer groups into the Apple ecosystem. This tiered pricing strategy is a textbook example of price architecture: the premium product frames everything else as accessible.

On the cost side, Apple systematically stripped variable costs from its products under the cover of sustainability initiatives. The removal of wired EarPods (iPhone 12, 2020), the charging brick (iPhone 12, 2020), and later the in-box stickers reduced per-unit material and packaging costs. Critically, these removals were reframed positively — as environmental responsibility — while simultaneously opening a new revenue stream: accessory upsells. AirPods, MagSafe chargers, and Apple-branded cables became high-margin incremental purchases that would previously have been bundled for free.

On the volume side, Apple expanded its addressable market through product differentiation and geographic pricing. Services (App Store, iCloud, Apple TV+, Apple Music) added a recurring revenue layer that grows with the user base independent of hardware unit sales.

The combination always beats the best single lever.
When you pull all three levers together — as Apple has done deliberately across product generations — the profit gain exceeds any single-lever strategy. Price remains the most powerful isolated driver, but combining a price increase with volume growth and cost reduction creates a compounding effect that no single lever can match.

Profit driver Profit impact of 1% improvement Improvement needed to double profit
Price11.0%9.1%

Source: McKinsey & Company, "The Price Advantage" (Marn, Roegner & Zawada). Study of 1,200 companies across industries. A 1% improvement in price yields an average 11% improvement in operating profit — nearly 3× the impact of a 1% improvement in unit sales. To double profit through price alone requires only a 9.1% increase; achieving the same through volume growth requires 27.1% more units sold.
